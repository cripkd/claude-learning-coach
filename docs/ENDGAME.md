# Endgame Playbook — Final 72 Hours (and the Week Before)

> This is the end-of-prep protocol. Most study plans are silent on the endgame. This file is what makes the prep feel complete.
>
> The anti-pattern the playbook is designed to prevent: arriving at exam day having studied right up until midnight, sleeping badly, and second-guessing every answer you'd already internalized.

---

## Last week (Days -7 through -4)

**Objective:** Consolidate, don't acquire.

- **Drill the Repeat-Miss Watchlist exclusively.** No new material. Every new topic you introduce in the last week is a liability: it competes for retrieval bandwidth with things you've already drilled, and it almost certainly won't be covered well enough to answer confidently.
- **Use the watchlist as your only study surface.** Open `misses.md`, go to the Watchlist section, and run through each item. Can you answer the diagnostic question without hesitation? If yes: done. If no: drill it once more, mark it, move on.
- **One short practice set per day maximum.** 10–15 questions, timed. The goal is to confirm reflex speed, not to learn anything new.
- **Update `CALIBRATION.md` cold-water estimate** after each practice set. Watch whether confidence and accuracy are converging.

---

## Day -3

**Objective:** One final shape-diverse confidence check + trap reflex audit.

- Run a 15–20 question set that covers all domains, with deliberate shape diversity (short-correct answers, scope-qualifier traps, cross-domain scenarios, anti-overapplication questions).
- After the set: for each miss, ask — is this a known watchlist item, or something new?
  - **Known watchlist miss:** drill it once more tonight. You need one more clean rep.
  - **New miss two days out:** note it, drill it once, do NOT spiral. Discovering one new edge case 72 hours before the exam is normal and manageable.
- Confirm your cold-water estimate. Update `state.json` readiness block.
- Identify any remaining trap reflexes that are not yet automatic. Note them for Day -2.

---

## Day -2

**Objective:** Narrow-target final drilling (~30 minutes, no more).

- Take the list from Day -3: the trap reflexes that aren't yet automatic.
- Drill only those items. One focused pass: read the diagnostic question, answer it out loud, confirm the mental model, move on.
- Do a light `cheatsheet.md` pass — not to memorize, but to confirm the decision rules feel natural and reachable.
- **Hard stop at 30 minutes.** If you're not done, you're drilling the wrong things. The point is narrow, targeted activation — not a review marathon.
- Rest. Eat well. Go to bed on schedule.

---

## Day -1 (rest day)

**No studying. This is a rule, not a suggestion.**

- No new material.
- No practice questions.
- No re-reading source docs.
- A brief scan of `cheatsheet.md` is acceptable (≤10 minutes) if you genuinely can't resist, but it should feel like a warm handshake with material you already know — not a study session.
- **Sleep schedule normalization:** go to bed at the time you need to wake up feeling rested, not at your usual time. If the exam is early, shift your sleep window on Day -1.
- Physical activity is helpful. Screens are not.

**Why:** Your brain consolidates during sleep. The last night before the exam is when that consolidation happens for the material you drilled on Day -2. Studying on Day -1 interrupts that process and replaces consolidation with anxiety.

## Exam day

### Time budget

Allocate your time before you start the clock:

- **Target ≤ N seconds per question**, where N = (total exam time in seconds) / (total questions) × 0.85. The 0.85 factor reserves ~15% of total time for the second pass and flagged items.
- Do the math in advance and write it on scratch paper at the start.

### First pass: flag-and-return

- Read each question. If you recognize the answer instantly: mark it and move on. Do not second-guess instant recognition.
- If you're uncertain: flag the question, make your best guess (never leave it blank), and move on. Do not spend more than 60 seconds on any single question on the first pass.
- The first pass should be fast. Momentum matters.

### Second pass: flagged items only

- Return only to flagged questions. Do not revisit questions you answered confidently.
- For each flagged item: apply the relevant decision rule from `cheatsheet.md` (from memory). If two options are still competing, ask: which one would the exam's mental model prefer? The exam rewards the cleanest application of the declared principle, not the most complete engineering solution.
- If you're still stuck: go with your first instinct. Switching answers on a re-read rarely improves score and often hurts it.

### Trust instant recognition

The most dangerous exam-day failure mode: you read a question, your trained pattern recognition fires immediately, you see the answer — and then a scope-qualifier word in the question makes you doubt yourself and switch to a wrong answer.

**The rule:** when a framing word makes you doubt instant recognition, ask yourself: does this word name a *different mechanism*, or just the *surface* where the mechanism you recognized applies? If it's surface-only, trust the recognition.

This reflex was drilled in practice. Trust it.

## Anti-patterns (things that reliably hurt exam performance)

- **Cramming new material on exam eve.** New material in the last 24 hours is undrilled material. It adds retrieval competition without adding confidence.
- **Ignoring rest.** A 5% accuracy penalty from fatigue is larger than anything you could learn in the last 12 hours of studying.
- **Second-guessing instant recognition.** See above. The pattern fires for a reason.
- **Spending too long on hard questions.** A question you spend 4 minutes on is worth exactly the same as a question you answer in 20 seconds. Flagging and returning is always better than stalling.
- **Treating the cold-water estimate as a ceiling.** The estimate is a calibration tool, not a prediction. Students routinely score above their cold-water estimate on exam day because test conditions (time pressure, no distractions) activate trained reflexes better than practice sessions do.
- **Ignoring the endgame protocol.** The protocol exists because these failure modes are real and common. The students who arrive at exam day well-rested with their watchlist drilled have a structural advantage over students who crammed until midnight.
